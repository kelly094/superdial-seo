# Prior Authorization Software in 2026: Causes, Costs, and How to Fix It

If your RCM team spends hours every week chasing prior authorizations, you are not alone, and the problem is getting worse. Payer requirements have expanded steadily, and Medicare Advantage plans in particular continue drawing scrutiny for using prior authorization as a cost-control mechanism rather than a clinical one (HFMA, 2025). Meanwhile, CMS is pushing hard for electronic prior authorization adoption, with new rules taking effect for Medicare Advantage and Part D plans that require FHIR-based ePrior authorization implementation by 2027 (CMS, 2024).

For billing teams in the middle of this, the pressure is real. Staff are tied up navigating payer portals, sitting on hold, and managing follow-up on approvals that should have been automatic. The result is delayed care, slower revenue, and burnout.

Here is what is driving the problem, and how modern prior authorization software addresses it.

## Why Prior Authorization Is So Difficult to Manage

The core issue is not that prior auth is complicated. It is that the process is fragmented, inconsistent, and largely manual at most organizations.

Payers use different submission channels, different clinical criteria, and different timelines. What one payer accepts electronically, another requires by phone or fax. Staff have to context-switch constantly, and each handoff creates risk: a missed attachment, a wrong form version, or a follow-up that falls through the cracks.

The burden lands hardest on patients with complex, ongoing conditions. KFF found that four in ten insured adults with a chronic condition identify prior authorization as their single biggest healthcare burden beyond direct costs (KFF, 2025). When authorization stalls, treatment stalls, and providers absorb the downstream cost through rescheduling, appeals, and bad debt.

Add to that the administrative overhead. Prior auth is one of the highest-touch workflows in the revenue cycle, yet much of the work is routine: checking status, submitting supporting documentation, logging payer responses. These are exactly the tasks that drain staff capacity without requiring clinical judgment.

## 5 Ways Prior Authorization Software Fixes the Problem

### 1. Automate Routine Submissions Across Payers

The highest-impact change most teams can make is eliminating manual submission for standard requests. Modern prior authorization software connects to payer portals, APIs, and EDI channels to submit requests without staff intervention. Where a portal or API is not available, voice-capable AI can navigate payer phone lines and IVR systems directly.

AI startups are increasingly competitive here. Companies including Cohere Health and EliseAI are building systems designed to handle payer conversations and clinical criteria matching at scale (Modern Healthcare, 2025). The differentiation increasingly lies not in whether a platform can submit a request, but in whether it can handle the full workflow: submission, status checks, follow-up, and documentation.

### 2. Centralize Status Tracking in One Structured View

One of the biggest sources of staff time loss is checking authorization status. A request goes in, and then someone has to follow up in three days, and again in five, and again after that. This polling loop consumes hours per week across a typical billing team.

Prior authorization software replaces that loop with automated status retrieval. Staff see current approval status, expected decision timelines, and any missing information requirements, without making a single call. Every interaction is timestamped and auditable.

### 3. Flag Denials Before They Become Appeals

Denial prevention is more valuable than denial appeals. Software that matches clinical documentation against payer criteria before submission can catch mismatches early, prompt for the right supporting records, and substantially reduce first-pass denial rates.

This is where AI-assisted review is gaining ground. Platforms like SmarterDx are applying AI to documentation review specifically to identify gaps that are likely to trigger denials, reducing the volume of cases that require appeals workflows (Modern Healthcare, 2025). For high-volume specialties, that upstream intervention has a direct impact on days in AR.

### 4. Integrate ePrior Authorization With EHR and Clinical Workflows

The CMS electronic prior authorization rule requires that impacted plans implement FHIR-based ePrior authorization by 2027, with the goal of reducing friction at the point of care (CMS, 2024). Software that is already integrated with EHR data can pull the relevant clinical information automatically, reducing the burden on clinical staff to re-enter information for every request.

This integration matters because prior auth delays often originate in the clinical workflow: a physician order is placed, but the supporting documentation does not reach the RCM team in time, or arrives incomplete. Software that bridges clinical and billing workflows closes that gap.

### 5. Build an Auditable Record for Every Request

Prior authorization disputes require documentation: what was submitted, when, what the payer responded, and what follow-up occurred. Without structured records, teams reconstruct timelines from memory or incomplete notes. That is a losing position in a payer dispute or a compliance review.

Good prior authorization software returns a complete audit trail for every case: submission timestamps, payer responses, call recordings where applicable, and next-action prompts. SuperDial customers report a 90% reduction in payer call time on prior auth workflows, with every interaction logged and retrievable for audit purposes. For billing companies managing multiple provider clients, that traceability is also a service quality differentiator.

## What to Look For in Prior Authorization Software

Not every platform handles the full workflow. When evaluating options, focus on:

- **Channel coverage:** Can it handle phone, portal, API, and fax? Or only one channel?
- **Structured outputs:** Does it return data in a usable format, or just a PDF or call summary?
- **Audit trail:** Are timestamps, transcripts, and payer responses captured automatically?
- **Denial prevention:** Does it flag documentation gaps before submission?
- **Scalability:** Can it absorb volume increases without proportional headcount growth?

Organizations using SuperDial have documented 4x more throughput on payer workflows without adding staff, which matters if your team is currently managing authorization volume growth by hiring.

## The Bottom Line

Prior authorization is not going to get simpler. Payer requirements will continue to expand, Medicare Advantage oversight will increase the documentation burden, and CMS mandates will push the industry toward electronic workflows that many teams are not yet equipped to handle.

The teams that come out ahead will be the ones that automate the routine, centralize the tracking, and build auditable processes that hold up under scrutiny. Prior authorization software is how that happens.
